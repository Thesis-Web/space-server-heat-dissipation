Thought for 19s
You’ve got several strong architectural instincts in here (zoning, thermal buffering, loop separation). But there’s one physics constraint that will dominate whether this can close:

The hard constraint: you can’t get 1200 K “for free” from a ~320 K heat source
If the primary heat source is GPU waste heat at ~300–360 K, you cannot elevate that same heat to ~800–1200 K (for a Brayton power cycle) without adding external work (heat pump behavior) or having an independent high-T source (solar concentrator, nuclear, etc.).

“Heat of compression” can raise gas temperature, yes.

But if you compress a working fluid using power that ultimately comes from the same low-grade heat you’re trying to upgrade, you run into exergy limits (Carnot). Net, you don’t magically gain useful power—at best you’re spending electrical power to lift radiator temperature (reducing area), i.e. a heat pump, not a generator.

So the right framing is:

Option A (Power cycle): Brayton generates power only if you have a hot source already at high T.

Option B (Heat pump): Brayton-like compression consumes power to lift heat to higher T so radiators shrink.

Both are valid. But they’re different machines, different control logic, different failure trees.

Cold-side TEGs on cold plates: technically possible, usually net-negative
What you’d get
At the cold plate you’re talking something like:

hot side: 320–360 K

cold side: maybe 280–310 K (if you have a colder sink; often you don’t inside the bay)

ΔT realistically: 10–40 K unless you create a real cold sink

At that ΔT, TEG efficiency is typically well under a few percent, and power density is modest. You might harvest watts to tens of watts per high-ΔT location—if you can keep the cold side cold.

What it costs you
Every TEG layer adds:

thermal resistance (worsens junction temps)

mechanical complexity (stack pressure uniformity)

reliability risk (bond lines, radiation aging, cycling)

Better use-case: TEGs as “parasitic trickle” for sensors / housekeeping (valve logic, telemetry) where a few watts is meaningful and you can tolerate added ΔT.
Bad use-case: powering meaningful fans/pumps; those loads are usually too big to justify the thermal penalty.

If you want “solid-state parasitic power,” it’s usually better to harvest from your highest ΔT boundary, not the cold plate.

So yes, a TEG can slightly reduce the heat that must be rejected downstream by exactly the electrical power it produces (tiny in practice). But it still passes almost all the heat to the cold side.

Key point in space
A TEG only “works” (maintains ΔT) if you have a real sink to take heat away from its cold side.

In vacuum, your sink is radiation (or a radiator-backed cold plate).

If the cold side can’t reject heat, it warms up, ΔT collapses, and the TEG output falls toward zero.

So: TEGs don’t remove heat by themselves; they require a heat-rejection path just like any other component.

2) Gas-to-gas cold plate loop: valid, but you’re accepting a performance tax
Running He/Xe gas through cold plates can be made to work, and it avoids two-phase headaches in µg. But it’s typically inferior heat transfer versus liquid because:

gas has low density → low heat capacity per volume

to move the same watts: higher flow rates, higher pressure drop, or larger channels/HX area

larger pumps/compressors, more parasitic power

That’s not a show-stopper—just a trade:

Pros: simpler phase management, fewer “bubble pathologies”

Cons: larger HX geometry + higher pumping/compression work

Also: two-phase isn’t automatically “not fun” in zero-g anymore—capillary-driven two-phase systems (heat pipes / loop heat pipes) are literally designed for space. But if your reliability posture says “single-phase only,” gas loops are coherent.

3) “Radiation heat amplifier” / “exponentially amplify heat” to reach 800–1200 K
This is the central physics constraint:

You cannot convert low-temperature waste heat into a higher-temperature heat source without adding work.
That’s not a technology gap; it’s thermodynamics (exergy / 2nd law).

What is possible is:

Heat pump behavior: spend work to lift heat from ~320–360 K up to ~800–1000 K so the radiator shrinks.

Or power cycle behavior: if you already have a hot source at ~800–1200 K, extract work.

Your idea of using microwave/RF to “superheat a core” is just an electric heater. The question becomes: where does the electrical power come from?

Case A — RF/microwave power comes from harvested waste heat
If you generate electricity from the waste heat (TEG, ORC, etc.) and then use it to RF-heat a core, you’ve built a heat engine + resistive heater loop.

Net effect: you converted some low-grade heat to electricity (with losses), then back to heat (with more losses).

You can make a spot get “very hot,” but you have not increased the usable exergy of the total system.

You’ve almost always made the overall system worse than directly using that electricity to drive a real heat pump/compressor.

Case B — RF/microwave power comes from your electrical bus (solar/nuclear/etc.)
Then you’re adding external energy. That can absolutely make 800–1200 K easy, but you’re no longer “solving heat without adding heat”—you’re paying power/mass to do it.

So the “radiation heat amplifier” as described doesn’t exist as a free amplifier. What exists is a heat pump, and it will have a COP limit.

4) The correct way to do what you want (same spirit, physically consistent)
If your goal is “use the heat we already have, and shrink radiators”
You want a heat pump to lift temperature, not a generator.

Practical candidates in spacecraft terms:

Reverse Brayton / turbo-compressor heat pump (gas cycle)

Stirling-type heat pump / cryocooler architecture (not necessarily cryo; same principle)

Vapor-compression heat pump (two-phase; more complex but very effective)

What you gain:

Smaller radiator area because rejection temperature rises
What you pay:
