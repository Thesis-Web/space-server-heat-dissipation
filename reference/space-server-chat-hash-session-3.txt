Using only the “ratio” we just computed (sensible vs latent on the 70°F → normal boiling @ 1 atm basis):

R-717 (ammonia) has Sensible:Latent ≈ 1 : 5.29

R-12 has Sensible:Latent ≈ 1 : 3.35

So ammonia carries more heat in the phase change per unit sensible change than R-12 (latent dominates more).

What that means (and what it does not mean)
✅ What it does mean
If you’re doing a process where you:

bring liquid to saturation, then

evaporate it,

then R-717 gives you more “bang” from the phase change relative to the sensible part. On that narrow metric, it’s “better” for moving heat via evaporation/condensation.

❌ What it doesn’t mean
That ratio is not overall refrigerant “efficiency.” HVAC/system efficiency is mostly about COP, which depends on:

evaporating temperature and condensing temperature (pressure ratio / lift)

compressor isentropic efficiency

volumetric refrigeration capacity (mass flow per displacement)

heat exchanger approach temps, superheat/subcool

motor and drive losses

discharge temperature limits

real fluid properties across the whole cycle

Practical comparison: R-717 vs R-12 (system-level)
Even though your ratio doesn’t decide COP, it aligns with why ammonia is a strong performer:

R-717 typically achieves high COP and strong heat-transfer coefficients, but

it’s toxic, mildly flammable under some conditions, and incompatible with some materials (notably copper) → higher system complexity and safety constraints.

R-12 was popular historically because it was:

stable

nonflammable

compatible with many materials
…but it’s ozone-depleting and effectively phased out.

Bottom line
On the latent-dominance metric: R-717 “better” than R-12 (more latent per unit sensible, ~5.3× vs ~3.35×).

On real efficiency (COP): you can’t conclude from that ratio alone — but ammonia is commonly a top-tier performer when the system is designed for it.

# Node Class S — 50 kW Module (Baseline Spec)

**Status:** Draft v0.1  
**Purpose:** Atomic deployable compute module for orbital fleet scaling.  
**Design driver:** High-temperature thermal rejection with a **1200 K radiator target** via a segregated thermal backbone.

---

## 1. Top-level targets

| Parameter | Target |
|---|---:|
| Electrical bus (continuous) | 50 kW |
| Compute payload (continuous) | 40–44 kW |
| Spacecraft overhead + margin | 6–10 kW |
| Thermal rejection (steady state) | ~50 kW |
| Radiator effective temperature | 1200 K (target) |
| Architecture | Cold compute loop + high-T backbone loop (HX-coupled) |
| Initial launch compatibility | Falcon 9 / Falcon Heavy / Starship |

---

## 2. Mission role

- Orbital compute node designed to operate as a fleet component.
- Primary early revenue: AI inference / accelerator compute.
- Network philosophy: standard IP at payload boundary; optical crosslinks between nodes; TT&C separated from payload traffic.

---

## 3. Thermal architecture (segmented)

### 3.1 Thermal zones
- **Zone A — Compute Vault (Cold Loop):** 300–350 K class
- **Zone B — Heat Exchanger Interface:** cold-to-hot transfer boundary
- **Zone C — High-T Backbone:** 1000–1200 K class transport loop
- **Zone D — Radiator Field:** 1200 K effective emission

### 3.2 Control intent
- Maintain Zone A within narrow band (reduce thermal cycling).
- Zone C operates at high temperature for radiator mass/area minimization and exergy leverage.
- No direct exposure of electronics to Zone C temperatures.

---

## 4. Compute payload (accelerator-first)

### 4.1 GPU baseline (locked class)
- **NVIDIA H200 class**
- Preferred configuration: **600 W TDP variant** where available (thermal benefit)

### 4.2 Block model (design abstraction)
Define a **GPU Block** as the repeatable unit:
- 10 × H200 @ 600 W = 6.0 kW
- Host CPUs sized for lanes + IO (platform-dependent)
- Local RAM + NVMe for caching/checkpoints

### 4.3 50 kW module target configuration
- **6 × GPU Blocks** → ~36 kW GPU power
- Host + RAM + NVMe + fabric → ~8 kW
- Total compute payload → **~44 kW**

---

## 5. Power system (baseline)
- Solar generation sized for 50 kW continuous with degradation margin.
- Battery sized for eclipse continuity (orbit-dependent; not locked in this doc).

---

## 6. Networking (baseline)
- Intra-node: high-throughput fabric (100–400 GbE class).
- Inter-node: optical crosslinks (aggregate Tbps-class target at fleet scale).
- Payload interface: IP-native (no proprietary dependency).

---

## 7. Packaging & deployment (concept)
- Module designed to fit within a 5.2 m fairing diameter class.
- Radiator at 1200 K reduces required area drastically vs 350 K class, enabling compact radiator packaging.
- Fleet scales by replication; failure domain limited to one module.

---

## 8. Open items to lock next
1) Orbit choice (LEO/GEO; eclipse fraction)
2) Solar array stow/deploy method and areal density assumption
3) High-T working fluid selection (He/Xe vs liquid metal) + materials stack
4) Crosslink bandwidth target per node (Gbps/Tbps) based on service model
5) Radiation strategy: shielding mass vs redundancy vs error model

EOF

# Thermal Backbone — 1200 K Class (Draft v0.1)

**Goal:** Enable compact radiator area and high exergy while keeping compute electronics on a safe cold loop.

---

## 1. Requirements

| Item | Target |
|---|---:|
| Radiator effective temperature | 1200 K |
| Steady-state heat rejection (Node S) | 50 kW |
| Electronics cold loop | 300–350 K |
| Loop coupling | Heat exchanger (cold ↔ hot) |
| Single-fault intent | No hot-loop fault should directly overtemp electronics |

---

## 2. Architecture summary

**Zone A (Cold Loop):** cold plates on GPU/CPU → cold manifold → cold-to-hot HX  
**Zone C (Hot Backbone):** HX → hot transport loop → radiator manifold → radiator panels

The loops are intentionally segregated:
- Electronics never see hot-loop fluid or hot-loop pressures directly.
- Hot-loop leakage is handled as a containment and isolation problem, not a “keep running” problem.

---

## 3. Radiator sizing (order-of-magnitude)

Use Stefan–Boltzmann:

\[
Q = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4
\]

Assumptions:
- \(\varepsilon = 0.9\)
- \(T = 1200\ \text{K}\)

Solve for area:

\[
A = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon \sigma T^4}
\]

For \(Q = 50,000\ \text{W}\):

Result: **~1.5–2.0 m²** effective emitting area (order-of-magnitude).

Notes:
- View factor, fin efficiency, non-uniform temperatures, and emissivity degradation can drive practical area upward.
- Keep a design margin (e.g., 2–3×) until materials/coatings and geometry are locked.

---

## 4. Working fluid candidates (hot loop)

### 4.1 He/Xe Brayton-class gas
**Pros**
- Compatible with turbine conversion architectures
- Lower chemical reactivity

**Cons**
- Requires compression (parasitic power)
- Lower volumetric heat capacity

### 4.2 Liquid metals (Na / NaK)
**Pros**
- High thermal conductivity and high heat transport capacity

**Cons**
- Reactivity/containment complexity
- Materials compatibility and corrosion risk

### 4.3 Molten salts
**Pros**
- Thermal storage capability

**Cons**
- Many salts have temperature limits and corrosion complexities
- Pumping and freeze/thaw management are non-trivial

---

## 5. Materials & radiator construction (directional)

At ~1200 K class, candidate structural and radiator materials include:
- Refractory metals (e.g., Mo, Nb alloys, W) depending on exact operating limits
- Carbon–carbon composites and high-temperature ceramics where applicable

Key risks:
- Emissivity stability at temperature over mission life
- Thermal stress / creep
- Micrometeoroid damage tolerance

---

## 6. Failure containment concepts

- Hot-loop isolation valves (segment the radiator field)
- HX bypass and emergency dump to radiator reserve area
- Cold-loop PCM buffer sized to give software time to shed load
- Autonomous compute throttling on any hot-loop anomaly

---

## 7. Open items to lock next

1) Pick hot-loop fluid and nominal pressures
2) Pick HX approach (gas-gas vs liquid-gas vs liquid-liquid boundary)
3) Emissive coating or surface treatment strategy
4) Radiator segmentation count and leak tolerance model
5) Parasitic power budget for pumps/compressor

EOF

# Compute Sled — H200-Class GPU Block (Draft v0.1)

**Purpose:** Define a repeatable “GPU Block” used to compose Node Class S (50 kW).

---

## 1. Block definition (baseline)

A **GPU Block** is the atomic compute payload unit.

| Item | Baseline |
|---|---|
| GPUs | 10 × NVIDIA H200 (target 600 W config) |
| GPU power | ~6.0 kW |
| Host CPUs | 2 × high-lane server CPUs (platform dependent) |
| CPU power budget | ~0.5–0.8 kW |
| Memory | 1 TB DDR5 ECC (baseline) |
| Memory power budget | ~0.2–0.4 kW |
| Local NVMe | 4 × enterprise NVMe |
| Storage power budget | ~0.08–0.12 kW |
| Fabric | 100–400 GbE class intra-node |
| Fabric power budget | ~0.05–0.15 kW |
| Block subtotal (non-GPU) | ~1.0–1.5 kW |
| Block total | ~7.0–7.5 kW |

---

## 2. Block-to-node composition

Node Class S target:
- **6 × GPU Blocks**
- GPU total: ~36 kW
- Non-GPU total: ~6–9 kW
- Total compute payload: ~42–45 kW

---

## 3. Mechanical and thermal boundaries

- Cold plates on GPUs/CPUs tied to the cold loop manifold.
- No direct coupling of compute sled hardware to hot-loop fluid.
- Each block should be independently power-gated and thermally isolated to contain faults.

---

## 4. Reliability posture

- Treat GPUs as attrition-prone; design N+M redundancy at fleet level.
- Implement:
  - watchdog + reset domains
  - ECC everywhere possible
  - periodic memory scrub
  - fault quarantine at block granularity

---

## 5. Open items

1) Exact motherboard/backplane standard (HGX-like vs custom)
2) Lane mapping and switching strategy
3) Target memory per GPU for early service tiers
4) NVMe layout for checkpoint and cache patterns
5) Block-level EMI and grounding plan

EOF

# Compute Baseline — 50 kW Module (Class S)

**Status:** Draft v0.1  
**Intent:** Lock a repeatable compute bill-of-materials class for the 50 kW node.

## 1) Power budget envelope

| Subsystem | Target (kW) | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| GPUs | 30–40 | Accelerator-first payload |
| CPU + DRAM | 3–6 | Host + memory power (platform dependent) |
| NVMe / local storage | 0.5–2 | Cache/checkpoint, not long-term archive |
| Fabric / switching | 0.5–2 | NVLink/PCIe + Ethernet/optics PHYs |
| BMC / control / misc | 0.2–0.8 | Flight computer, monitoring, housekeeping |
| **Compute payload subtotal** | **~40–44** | Design target |
| Spacecraft overhead + margin | 6–10 | ADCS, TT&C, pumps, heaters, etc. |
| **Node total** | **50** | Electrical bus continuous |

## 2) Accelerator class (locked)

### 2.1 GPU class
- **NVIDIA H200 class**
- Target TDP band: **600–700 W** per GPU (bin-dependent; use 600 W where possible)
- Cooling: cold-loop cold plates (Zone A), heat lifted via HX to high-T backbone (Zone C)

### 2.2 GPU Block abstraction
Define one repeatable **GPU Block**:
- 10 × H200 @ 600 W → **6.0 kW** GPU power
- Host CPU(s) sized for lanes, IO, and memory bandwidth
- DRAM sized for model staging + caching
- NVMe for local dataset shards + checkpoints

## 3) Host CPU classes (options)

We do not lock a specific part number yet; we lock **capability classes**.

### Option A — x86 server host class
- Dual-socket class, high PCIe lane count (Gen5/Gen6 timeline dependent)
- RAS features: ECC, patrol scrub, MCA, memory mirroring (optional)
- Target host power per GPU Block: **0.5–1.2 kW**

### Option B — ARM server host class
- High efficiency, high core count, PCIe lane density varies by platform
- Target host power per GPU Block: **0.4–1.0 kW**

## 4) DRAM classes

- ECC mandatory
- Baseline per GPU Block: **1–4 TB** system RAM (range depends on workload)
- Memory bandwidth and error model must be documented (radiation upset strategy)

## 5) Storage classes

### NVMe local (hot)
- Baseline per GPU Block: **30–120 TB** NVMe (TLC) as cache/checkpoint

won't titanium handle 1200 k? 


Thought for 16s
1200 K (≈ 927 °C) is not a realistic long-duration “service temperature” for titanium in most engineering contexts.

What titanium can “handle”
Melting point: titanium melts around 1668 °C (≈ 1941 K). So 1200 K is below melt, yes.

But “below melt” ≠ “usable as a radiator/structure at that temperature.”

Why titanium is a bad choice at 1200 K
Strength + creep collapse well before 1200 K

A common aerospace alloy, Ti-6Al-4V, is typically recommended only up to about 400 °C (≈ 673 K) for sustained use. 

NASA work on commercially pure Ti highlights a maximum service temperature ~650 K (≈ 377 °C) in their context. 

Those are an order-of-magnitude away from 1200 K.

Oxidation / embrittlement is severe when oxygen is present

At ~900 °C class temps, titanium forms aggressive oxide/“alpha-case” layers and can embrittle rapidly.

In LEO, atomic oxygen is a real materials adversary; at 1200 K, you’re stacking worst-cases.

Radiator practicality

Radiators at 1200 K need stable emissivity and surface chemistry that won’t degrade. Titanium’s surface behavior and coatings at that temperature are nontrivial and usually not the path of least resistance.

What to use instead (depending on the loop + orbit)
Refractory metals / alloys: niobium alloys, molybdenum alloys, tungsten (high-temp capable, but density/oxidation considerations).

Ceramics / composites: SiC, carbon–carbon (excellent at high T; oxidation protection needed if oxygen present).

Architecture workaround: keep the radiator skin at very high T, but do not make it titanium load-bearing; use titanium only in cooler structures or isolated brackets.

Bottom line
Titanium won’t “handle 1200 K” in the sense you mean for a 1200 K radiator/backbone—it’s way beyond typical titanium service limits even if it won’t melt. 

If you tell me orbit (LEO vs GEO) and whether the radiator sees any oxidizing species (or is inside a sealed inert cavity), I’ll narrow the best material stack + coating strategy for your specific architecture.
