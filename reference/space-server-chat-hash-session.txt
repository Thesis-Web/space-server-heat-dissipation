Yes, you can interact with the sparse GEO environment, but no, you can’t realistically “increase the local gas density” around a spacecraft in a way that’s useful for heat rejection (or anything like convection).

Here’s the physics breakdown.

What’s actually “out there” at GEO
At GEO altitudes you’re in the exosphere / magnetosphere regime:

Particles are mostly neutral hydrogen (H) and helium (He) plus a very tenuous plasma (ions + electrons) from the magnetosphere.

Number densities are extremely low (orders of magnitude too low for any meaningful “atmosphere-like” behavior).

Mean free paths are enormous → particles don’t behave like a fluid.

So even if you could “pull more in,” you’re still in a collisionless regime.

Can a material “attract” neutral H / He?
For neutral atoms: basically no
Neutral H and He do not respond strongly to static fields. You only get:

Van der Waals / polarization forces (very short range, microscopic)

Cryosorption at very low temperatures (capture, not densify)

Chemical gettering for hydrogen (binds/absorbs it), but helium is inert

These don’t create a meaningful “cloud” around you. They just stick what hits the surface.

Hydrogen is special (sort of)
Some materials absorb hydrogen (getters / hydrides):

Titanium, zirconium, vanadium-based getters (SAES-type getters)

Palladium absorbs hydrogen (mostly as an equilibrium absorber)

Many metals uptake hydrogen at certain temperatures

But again: this acts like a pump/sink, not a “density amplifier.” You’d reduce local density, not increase it.

Helium? Almost nothing “attracts” helium chemically.

Can you use fields to pull in particles?
If particles are ionized: you can deflect/collect them
If you have plasma ions/electrons, then:

Electric fields can attract/repel charged particles

Magnetic fields can guide charged particles

But limits kill you:

Debye shielding: plasmas rearrange to cancel fields beyond a small distance scale.

Flux is tiny: you can’t collect enough mass flow to matter thermally.

Energy cost: ionizing neutrals to make them collectible costs power and still doesn’t yield useful mass flow.

There are concepts like “plasma brakes,” “electric sails,” and charged tethers, but those work (when they do) by interacting with solar wind / ionospheric plasma and primarily for momentum exchange—not for creating a dense working fluid.

At GEO, plasma density is not in the regime where you can build a “gas jacket” around a vehicle.

The key constraint: you can’t get convection cooling this way
To move hundreds of kW via convective heat transfer you need mass flow and collisions (a real fluid). GEO simply doesn’t provide enough particles.

Even a “perfect collector” would intercept an insignificant particle flux unless your collection cross-section is absurdly large (kilometers-scale), and even then it’s not a stable atmosphere—it’s a sparse collisionless stream.

What is feasible and potentially useful
1) Getters / cryosurfaces for contamination control
Remove outgassing products

Keep optics/thermal coatings clean

Manage internal vacuum volumes

Useful, but not a heat-rejection mechanism.

2) Charging control / plasma interaction
Controlling spacecraft charging

ESD risk reduction

Potential small forces/torques

Again: not thermal.

Field attraction: works only on plasma; GEO plasma is too tenuous and shields fields; flux is far too small.

No practical way to “increase local density” around you in GEO to create a usable external working fluid.

Can the TPV be the radiator surface?
Only in a qualified sense:

What TPV “radiator” really looks like
A TPV system has two distinct thermal surfaces:

Hot emitter (radiates photons at high T)

PV cells (absorb photons, make electricity, and become warm)

The PV cells need to be kept relatively cool to work well, which means they require a cold-side heat sink—i.e., a radiator.

So you typically end up with:

Hot emitter inside

PV array facing it

PV backside bonded to a radiator panel (or heat pipes to one)

The “radiator” is still there—it’s just now thermally attached to the PV.

The temperature problem (critical)
TPV efficiency depends heavily on emitter temperature and spectral matching.

At 800–1000 K, TPV efficiency is generally poor (photon energies are too low, spectrum not well matched to practical PV bandgaps; lots of sub-bandgap photons become waste heat).

TPV becomes more compelling when the emitter is very hot (often >1200 K, and more realistically 1500–2000 K for strong performance depending on emitter/PV tech).

But your system is trying to reject waste heat originating near 300–350 K. If you have to raise that to 1200–1600 K to make TPV worthwhile, you’re back to paying the heat-pump / exergy tax.

When TPV helps in your GEO node
TPV is useful if you already have a genuinely hot thermal domain, e.g.:

Solar concentrator receiver

Very high-temperature radiator backbone you were building anyway

High-grade heat source (which you previously allowed only if you accept concentrator)

In that case TPV can:

Recover some electrical power,

Potentially power pumps/controls,

Reduce required solar array a bit.

But it still won’t eliminate the radiator, because:

PV waste heat must be rejected,

non-converted photons are still heat somewhere.

Thought for 21s
Starlink “deals with it” the boring way: they radiate it—but they’re operating in a regime where that’s easy because the wattage per spacecraft is kW-class, not data-center class.

Below is the closest-to-actionable translation of Starlink-style thermal to a 50 kW GEO node.

What Starlink actually does (mechanisms, not hype)
1) Passive rejection via radiator surfaces
Spacecraft reject internal dissipation by putting heat onto surfaces with high IR emissivity and low solar absorptivity (“radiator properties”). NASA’s smallsat thermal-control guidance is basically the playbook here. 

2) Heat spreading / transport to those surfaces
Starlink is widely believed (and consistent with SpaceX patent filings) to use heat pipes / thermally-coupled radiator panels to spread heat and avoid local hot spots, rather than exotic heat engines. For example, SpaceX-related patents describe thermally coupling radiator panels and using space-structure thermal management with heat pipes. 

3) Attitude + layout control
They can point/operate so that “radiator sides” have a good view to cold space and avoid direct solar loading as much as possible (standard practice; not unique to Starlink). 

Net: No Brayton, no heat pumps, no TPV required. Just: spread → conduct/pipe → radiate.

Why Starlink gets away with it
The scaling law is brutal
Radiation capacity at modest temperatures is only hundreds to a few thousand W/m². So once you leave “kW class” and enter “50 kW / 300 kW,” the radiator becomes the spacecraft.

NASA’s thermal-control overview frames radiators/coatings/MLI as the core knobs—because in vacuum, there is no alternative sink. 

Scaling Starlink-style thermal up to a 50 kW GEO node
Let’s assume you want fixed panels / body skin, no deployables.

Radiator area ideal (ε≈0.9, one-sided, perfect view) from Stefan–Boltzmann:

600 K: ~7.6 m² for 50 kW

800 K: ~2.4 m²

1000 K: ~1.0 m²

The “what breaks first” list when you scale up
1) View factor + solar/earth loading (geometry problem)
In GEO, some portion of any fixed surface will see:

Sun (direct + reflected)

Earth IR / albedo (geometry dependent)

internal self-viewing

So you never get “perfect deep-space sink.” Your effective required area is bigger than the ideal equation unless you can keep radiator faces shaded and with good sky view. NASA explicitly treats radiator sizing as coupled to coatings and environment. 

2) Hot-skin vs cold-electronics isolation
If you run the skin at 800–1000 K to shrink area, keeping electronics at 300–345 K becomes a zoning + conduction fight:

stand-offs / low-k structural paths

MLI where appropriate

intentional heat paths only through designed HX/heat pipes

This is manageable, but it becomes the main architecture.

3) Transport inside the node
Starlink-class systems can use heat pipes effectively because the heat fluxes and distances are reasonable. At 50 kW, you start needing:

many parallel transport paths

serious spreading plates

redundancy (for “100% uptime”)

SpaceX’s “radiator coupling / structural thermal management” patent direction is consistent with exactly this scaling approach: distribute heat and share radiating surfaces. 

Practical “Starlink-to-50 kW GEO” recipe (best baseline)
If you want simple + uptime + no deployables, the cleanest baseline is:

Make the spacecraft body the radiator (smooth panels, no fins)

Choose a credible radiator skin temperature:

~600–700 K (easier materials, larger area)

~800–900 K (aggressive but plausible)

~1000 K (very aggressive; materials/coatings dominate risk)

Use distributed heat pipes / pumped loops to spread load to multiple faces

Use radiator coatings with “radiator properties” (low α, high ε) where those faces can be kept shaded from the Sun 

Enforce thermal zoning so hot skin doesn’t back-drive into the compute bay

That is the scalable version of what Starlink is doing, just pushed into a higher power regime.
