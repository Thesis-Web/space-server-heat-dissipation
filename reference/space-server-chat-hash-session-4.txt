Thought for 15s
Yes—dielectric (“non-conductive”) immersion cooling liquids exist, and what you’re describing is basically single-phase immersion cooling (electronics bathed in a dielectric fluid, pumped through a heat exchanger into your PCM / eutectic-metal thermal battery).

The question isn’t “does a fluid exist?”—it’s whether the mass, contamination control, materials compatibility, and microgravity fluid management beat your He/Xe architecture.

1) Do suitable liquids exist?
A) Hydrocarbon / synthetic oil class (generally the best “space-realistic” starting point)
Examples: PAO (polyalphaolefin), highly refined mineral oils, some synthetic esters used as dielectric coolants.

Pros

Good dielectric strength (when dry/clean)

Lower density than fluorinated fluids (mass advantage)

Widely used in immersion cooling on Earth; lots of materials data

Cons

Flammability / fire behavior (mitigated in vacuum but still a hazard during integration/testing)

Can interact with certain elastomers, plastics, conformal coatings over time (needs compatibility screening)

(General immersion cooling performance/reliability is well-studied, but long-life compatibility still matters.) 

B) Fluorinated fluids (historically popular; now complicated)
Examples: legacy 3M Novec / Fluorinert, and PFPEs like Solvay Galden.

Pros

Excellent dielectric behavior, non-flammable, very stable

Cons

High density (mass penalty)

Supply/regulatory uncertainty: 3M is exiting PFAS manufacturing by end of 2025, and Novec production cessation is widely reported in the supply chain. 

PFPE alternatives exist, but you’re still in “specialty fluid” territory (procurement + qualification burden). 

C) “PFAS-free engineered dielectric fluids”
There are vendors marketing biodegradable / PFAS-free single-phase immersion fluids. These may be viable, but you’d treat them like any other oil: validate outgassing, radiation stability, and compatibility (not just data-center claims). 

2) Would it work thermally (vs He/Xe)?
The big win: volumetric heat capacity
Liquids carry far more heat per unit volume than a gas. That means:

Lower volumetric flow for the same kW

Smaller ducts / less fan/compressor complexity

More uniform board temperatures (less “hot gas boundary layer” issues)

This is why immersion cooling is so effective for high heat flux electronics on Earth. 

The big cost: mass and plumbing realities
If you immerse a “rack/module” in liquid, you’re committing to:

tank mass + fluid mass + pump(s) + filters + degasser + accumulator

For spacecraft, that becomes a packaging and reliability game:

leaks are catastrophic

pump redundancy becomes mandatory

bubble management is non-trivial in microgravity

Net: it will cool extremely well, but you may lose your mass advantage unless you’re very disciplined about module sizing and radiator/PCM integration.

3) Contamination control: how do we keep the bath clean and non-conductive?
You don’t “filter ions” the way you would in water loops. You control contamination by keeping the fluid dry, clean, and chemically stable.

Practical contamination controls (what you’d actually build)
Closed sealed loop (no headspace exposed to anything)

Particulate filtration

inline absolute filters (micron-class) to catch wear particles, solder balls, connector debris

Degassing / gas management

a membrane degasser or dedicated degas volume + pump sequencing

microgravity gas bubbles don’t “rise” out; you must actively trap/remove them

Moisture control

internal desiccant / getter cartridge and strict bakeout/assembly

water is the silent killer for dielectric strength

Chemical cleanliness

choose compatible elastomers, seals, wire jackets, conformal coatings

avoid materials that leach plasticizers into the bath

Inline condition monitoring

dielectric strength proxy (often resistivity/leakage measurement)

particle count trending

acid number / oxidation indicators for oil-type fluids

Reliability/material interactions in oil immersion systems are a known engineering topic (not hand-wavy). 

4) Would it be too heavy?
Maybe. The answer depends on what “module” you immerse.

If you immerse the entire data-board bay:
Fluid volume is huge → mass explodes → launch cost pain.

If you immerse small sealed compute bricks (tight volume, high packing factor):
Fluid mass is contained

You get the thermal benefits where they matter (GPU VRMs, memory, hotspots)

You can keep the rest of the spacecraft “dry” and conventional

A good heuristic: immersion makes sense when it lets you delete enough other hardware (cold plates, TIM stacks, large gas plenums, compressors) to offset the tank+fluid+pumps.

5) Would it work with your PCM + eutectic-metal secondary?
Yes. The clean architecture is:

Primary loop (dielectric bath): boards immersed, pumped circulation

Primary-to-secondary HX: compact plate or microchannel exchanger (keep it serviceable / redundant)

Secondary loop: your eutectic metal PCM thermal battery / high-T backbone

This preserves your “thermal nervous system” concept while making Zone 1 brutally effective at heat pickup.

6) The “gotchas” specific to space
These are the issues that decide the trade:

Microgravity two-phase behavior: you want single-phase liquid in the board bath. Boiling/condensing in microgravity gets ugly fast unless you’re designing a full two-phase system intentionally.

Outgassing + contamination film risk: any film on optics/radiators is a mission-killer.

Radiation + chemistry: long-term radiation exposure can change fluid properties; you need test data for your orbit and shielding.

Pump lifetime: you’re swapping a Brayton compressor/fan complexity for pumps + seals + wear particles.

Bottom line recommendation
If you want to pursue this seriously, the best “out-of-the-box but not crazy” variant is:

sealed immersion “compute bricks” (not the whole spacecraft bay)

single-phase dielectric oil-class fluid (PAO / ester family) as baseline

aggressive cleanliness + degassing + filtration

redundant pump pair per brick class (N+1) with isolation valves

interface to your PCM + eutectic-metal hub through a serviceable HX

Avoid building around discontinued/supply-uncertain fluids (e.g., legacy Novec/Fluorinert class) unless you lock procurement and qualification early; 3M’s PFAS exit timeline is a real program risk.

Thought for 8s
You’re mostly wrong on the “it’s hard to make water in space” premise—not because water is being synthesized onboard from Ti/Al, but because water is almost impossible to eliminate in a real spacecraft/electronics assembly.

1) Why water shows up anyway (even in a hermetic compartment)
Water isn’t mainly “made”; it’s already there and it comes back out
Even if you avoid “oxygen-containing components” (which is effectively impossible for modern electronics), you still get water from:

Adsorbed moisture on surfaces (metals, ceramics, PCB glass fiber, powders, fillers). This is huge in practice.

Outgassing/desorption from:

epoxies, underfills, conformal coatings

solder mask, PCB laminates (FR-4 is glass + epoxy; it holds water)

wire insulation, potting compounds

elastomers (as you already flagged)

Assembly contamination: rinse solvents, humidity exposure, flux residues, fingerprints, cleaning agents.

Your “pure element” bill-of-materials is not realistic
PCBs are oxygen-rich (glass + epoxy) by definition.

Most dielectrics and package materials are oxides/nitrides (SiO₂, Al₂O₃, glass, etc.).

Titanium and aluminum themselves are covered in stable oxide layers immediately. You’re not keeping oxygen out of the system just by choosing Ti/Al; the oxide is inherent.

So the right mental model is:

“Hermetic” reduces ingress, but you still fight water as a residual + outgassing product unless you bake, purge, getter, and verify.

2) Elastomer leaching really is a problem child (you’re right)
In an immersion/dielectric loop, the top reliability killers are usually:

plasticizer / oligomer leaching → fluid contamination → lower resistivity / deposits on hot parts

swelling / embrittlement of seals

additive depletion (for formulated oils)

particulates from wear + assembly debris

This is why “space-grade” versions tend to:

minimize elastomers

prefer welded/brazed joints

use metal C-seals / spring-energized PTFE where possible

keep polymers out of the hot zone

3) “Put the pumps inside” — yes, that’s the right direction
You can absolutely reduce leak surfaces by using a fully hermetic pump:

Pump architectures that help
Canned-motor centrifugal (motor isolated behind a thin metal can)

Mag-drive centrifugal (no shaft seal, but you still have bearings and internal clearances)

Wet-rotor BLDC (motor designed to run in the fluid)

Gear / screw pumps (positive displacement; better with entrained gas than pure centrifugal)

You still need:

electrical feedthroughs (hermetic connectors)

heat rejection path for motor losses

filtration because pumps create wear products

So yes: internal pump reduces external plumbing and seal count, but it doesn’t delete “leak risk”—it moves it to welds + feedthroughs + internal joints.

4) “Pumps that don’t care about bubbles” — partially true
Positive displacement pumps (gear, screw) are more tolerant of entrained gas than centrifugal pumps, but they don’t make bubbles harmless. Gas still causes:

loss of effective flow / pressure (compressible pockets)

torque ripple / speed instability

localized heating and sometimes cavitation-like damage where pressure drops

measurement errors (flow meters, pressure sensors, thermal sensors)

If you need “bubble-tolerant,” you design for:

entrained gas fraction limits

gas handling strategy (see below)

no-vapor-pressure excursions (avoid flashing)

5) How bubbles hurt you in microgravity (the real list)
In 0g, bubbles don’t “float away.” They stay where flow, surface tension, and geometry put them. That creates several failure modes:

A) Thermal performance collapse at hot spots
A bubble sitting on a heat source creates an insulating patch, raising junction temps and causing runaway in the worst case.

B) “Gas lock” and loss of prime
Bubbles collect at high points (as defined by geometry/flow), creating persistent voids that a pump can’t clear. With a centrifugal pump, that can mean no flow.

C) Cavitation / flashing risk (even without gravity)
Cavitation is about local pressure vs vapor pressure, not gravity. If any region drops below vapor pressure, you generate vapor cavities. In microgravity those cavities can persist and grow.

D) Dielectric breakdown risk where bubbles concentrate electric field
Gas voids in a dielectric liquid can reduce breakdown strength locally (field enhancement), especially near sharp conductors, connectors, or HV sections.

E) Sensor/control instability
Two-phase flow ruins:

mass flow inference

pressure drop correlations

heat exchanger effectiveness predictions
Your control loops start hunting.

6) The practical fix: you don’t “hope bubbles behave,” you design a gas-management element
You add one (or more) of these inside the sealed system:

Gas accumulator / ullage trap with capillary acquisition (a deliberate “bubble parking lot”)

Vortex / swirl separator (centrifugal separation using flow)

Hydrophobic membrane degasser (lets gas permeate to a trapped volume)

Bubble trap geometry: screens, step expansions, and wettability control to capture gas

In microgravity, wettability and capillary structures are your “gravity replacement.”

7) What I’d recommend for your concept (given your goals)
If you want immersion to be your Zone-1 heat pickup:

Use a single-phase dielectric liquid loop

Put hermetic pumps inside the module

Add a purpose-built gas trap/degasser + accumulator

Minimize elastomers; qualify any remaining ones with soak tests at temperature

Use inline particulate filtration + condition monitoring (resistivity proxy + particle count trend)

Keep HV sections isolated or potted if you have any significant electric fields

You’re comparing two complicated solutions that fail in different ways:

He/Xe forced-gas convection loop (pressurized electronics bay, fans/compressor, gas-to-PCM/eutectic exchanger)

Single-phase dielectric immersion liquid loop (boards in dielectric bath, internal pumps, liquid-to-PCM/eutectic exchanger)

Below is the engineering trade in the dimensions you asked for.

Winner by category (executive)
Pure heat transfer (per unit area / hotspot control): Immersion liquid

Product life (electronics stress + fewer wear mechanisms): Depends

If you can qualify long-duration fluid/material compatibility: Immersion wins

If you can’t: He/Xe wins (less chemistry risk)

Lower maintenance / operational simplicity (long unattended life): He/Xe (usually)

Lower mass (all-in, likely): He/Xe (usually)

Lowest leak risk at system level: He/Xe (typically fewer seals in the hot zone)

1) Heat transfer capability (what actually cools silicon better)
Why immersion wins thermally
Single-phase immersion liquids have much higher volumetric heat capacity and typically yield higher convective heat transfer coefficients at practical flow rates than gases. This is why immersion cooling is so effective in high-heat-flux electronics. 

Practical consequence:

Lower ΔT from junction → fluid bulk

Better hotspot suppression (VRMs, memory, package edges)

Less need for precision cold plates / TIM stacks (you may still want local spreaders, but not the same dependency)

What He/Xe does well
He and He/Xe mixtures are used because helium has high thermal conductivity for a gas, and pressurization + forced convection can be effective, especially in engineered channels. But it’s still a gas: your “h” and volumetric heat capacity are fighting physics compared to liquids. 

Thermal verdict: Immersion is better on raw heat pickup and hotspot control.

2) Life of product (electronics lifetime + component degradation)
Think in two buckets:

A) Thermal stress on silicon (Arrhenius-driven wear-out)
Lower junction temperatures and fewer thermal gradients generally extend life. Immersion tends to win here because it equalizes temps and reduces hotspots. 

B) Chemistry/material compatibility (slow killers)
Immersion introduces a new class of life limiters:

elastomer swelling/leaching

polymer softening (wire jackets, conformal coatings, potting)

deposits/films on hot surfaces

fluid degradation over time (esp. under radiation + heat)

The immersion literature is explicit that material compatibility and contamination management are central reliability constraints. 

He/Xe avoids most chemical interaction risks; its aging modes are more “mechanical” (fans/compressors, seals, bearings) and “particulate” rather than “chemistry + fluids.”

Life verdict:

If you can run minimal-elastomer, welded hermetic, qualified materials: Immersion can be best.

If not: He/Xe is safer because it removes a large uncertainty class.

3) Maintenance / operational burden (what breaks first)
He/Xe system maintenance drivers
moving parts: fans/compressor (bearings), valves

particulate management (filters)

pressure management / leak checking

But: gas loops are generally forgiving (no viscosity issues, less deposition risk, easier startup behavior).

Immersion system maintenance drivers
pumps (and pump wear debris)

filtration (more critical)

fluid property drift (dielectric strength proxy, contamination)

bubble/gas management (microgravity makes this non-negotiable)

compatibility-induced failures can be “quiet” until catastrophic

Microgravity two-phase/gas-liquid behavior is a known complexity multiplier: you need separators/traps/membranes, not “hope.” 

Maintenance verdict: He/Xe tends to be lower-maintenance / lower-surprise, unless you make the immersion module extremely disciplined (sealed compute brick, factory-filled, qualified).

4) Bubbles and microgravity risk (reliability killer potential)
This hits immersion harder:

Gas pockets don’t rise; they stick and can cause gas-lock, insulation over hotspots, sensor/control instability.

You must design in phase separation / gas extraction elements (capillary, centrifugal, membrane). 

He/Xe is single-phase gas; “bubbles” are not a concept—your flow control is simply easier.

Microgravity risk verdict: He/Xe is inherently cleaner.

5) Which is “better” overall?
If your top priority is max heat flux + simplest silicon thermal stack
Pick immersion, but do it as sealed immersion compute bricks, not “fill the whole bay,” and treat materials compatibility + gas management as first-class design drivers. 

If your top priority is fleet reliability + predictable life + minimal new failure modes
Pick He/Xe, because it avoids chemistry and microgravity gas-liquid headaches, and it scales with fewer “unknown unknowns.” (Your mechanical components remain the primary maintenance risk.)
